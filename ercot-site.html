Fuel-Price Passthrough on the Texas Grid, Measured by an Autonomous Research Loop

An LLM-driven system runs an empirical study end-to-end — proposing hypotheses, dispatching supercomputer simulations, and writing up findings — to answer a real risk question: how unevenly does a natural-gas price shock reach electricity prices across ERCOT?

Daniel Wu · UNC Institute for Risk Management · 2026
What this is

A method and a finding, deliberately fused

This is first a methods project and second a scientific study. The method is an autonomous, inference-only agentic research loop. The study it runs is the demonstrating case: gas→power price passthrough across the Texas grid.

Contribution 01

The loop itself

A working autonomous research loop on real HPC infrastructure (UNC Longleaf, SLURM, Gurobi), with explicit scientific guardrails that keep its output causal-in-the-model rather than merely plausible-sounding.

Contribution 02

The empirical finding

A validated, interpretable result produced by that loop: gas→power passthrough is sizable but strongly location-dependent, and the heterogeneity tracks each bus's generation mix and congestion exposure.

No model training is involved. The loop is inference-only — it uses Claude models as reasoning components but trains nothing. Learning across iterations happens through an explicit, inspectable knowledge-base ledger, not weight updates. The two ends of the loop are LLMs (propose science, write prose); the middle is rigorous deterministic machinery. Humans stay in a supervisory role — the honest framing is supervised autonomy.

The scientific question

When gas gets expensive, who actually pays?

When natural gas gets more expensive, electricity gets more expensive — but by how much, and is it the same everywhere? For each bus (network location) n, the passthrough coefficient measures how much of a gas-price shock reaches the Locational Marginal Price at that bus.

passthrough_n = mean_over_qualifying_hours( LMP_n_scenario − LMP_n_baseline ) / Δgas_price

The shock is in $/MMBtu (gas); the response is in $/MWh (power); the coefficient is effectively a heat rate.

CoefficientRegimeWhat it means
≈ 1.0Full passthroughGas is the marginal fuel — $1/MMBtu of gas maps to roughly one heat-rate's worth of $/MWh.
< 0.5InsulatedWind or hydro is often marginal, or congestion shields the bus from the shock.
> 1.0AmplifiedCongestion rent concentrates the shock at the bus.

The coefficient is a price sensitivity — a hedge ratio, a "delta." Because it differs by location, the dispersion across the grid is a form of basis risk: the same gas shock hits different nodes by different amounts, so a hedge calibrated to a hub can leave a position at another node mis-hedged. The goal is not just to measure average passthrough, but to explain why it varies.

The number decides, the LLM explains

Every hypothesis carries a machine-readable predicate, evaluated against the scenario's passthrough data to produce a deterministic verdict — confirmed, falsified, or inconclusive. The LLM then writes interpretation and caveats around that fixed verdict; it never grades itself.

One knob, held everything else fixed

The only input the agent ever modifies is the daily fuel-price file. Load, wind, solar, outages, hydro, topology, and generator parameters are held byte-identical across baseline and scenario by symlinking — so any price change is attributable to the gas shock alone.

The simulation backbone

GO_ERCOT — a day-ahead economic dispatch model

Each experiment is a full simulation of the ERCOT grid: a linear program built in Pyomo and solved with Gurobi, iterated day by day across a full year.

LMPs are the dual variables of the nodal power-balance constraint. Economically, an LMP approximates gas_price × heat_rate when a gas unit is marginal, sits near zero when wind or hydro is marginal, and diverges across buses when a transmission constraint binds. That divergence is the source of the spatial heterogeneity the study measures. The LP is deliberate: unit-commitment binaries would contaminate the dual-price interpretation with startup-cost artifacts.

Validation against reality

Honest about what the model captures

The simulated baseline is checked against observed 2019 ERCOT settlement prices before any finding is trusted. Price levels match well — roughly $30.6/MWh modeled versus $35.8/MWh real, load-weighted — but hourly correlation is only moderate (r ≈ 0.43), driven by scarcity spikes the simple LP does not reproduce. The study measures normal-times sensitivity cleanly and flags the scarcity tail separately, rather than letting a handful of extreme hours dominate a coefficient.
